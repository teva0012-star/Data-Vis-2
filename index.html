Defining Unemployment

Unemployment rate is defined as the number of people in working age that are currently unemployed but are actively looking for a job, divided by the total labor force. The unemployment rate is a lagging economic indicator, which means that it reacts to past circumstances, rather than predicting in which direction an economy may evolve.

Unemployment Between Genders

Men's Unemployment participation tends to remain relatively stable across life stages, as it is generally unaffected by parenthood. In contrast, women's unemployment rates decline from early adulthood onward, reflecting the disproportionate impact of childbearing and caregiving responsibilities. These responsibilities can lead to career interruptions, reduced working hours, or temporary withdrawal from the labor force.

Working Sector on Unemployment

As countries get richer, the share of the population working in agriculture tends to decline as people move towards employment in industry and services. That is why countries that have long been developed, such as the USA, Australia, and Luxembourg have an oversaturated service sector, while developing or more recently developed countries such as China, Brazil, and Eswatini have a much more even spread of unemployment rates across the three sectors.

Economic Growth

Economic growth refers to an increase in the size of a country's economy over a period of time. The size of an economy is typically measured by the total production of goods and services in the economy, which is called gross domestic product (GDP).

Note that GDP doesn't capture the broader aspects of economic welfare of a nation's population. For example, between 1992 and 1993, the AFrican GDP grew by 10 billion USD, and the population increased by 20 million people, which resulted in a 1.4% decrease in Africa's GDP per capita.

The Role of Population Growth in Economic Health

Population growth represents a significant factor in economic development. An increasing population has the potential to expand the labor force, which, when effectively managed, can drive economic growth and enhance productivity. However, if not adequately addressed, rapid population growth may place considerable strain on infrastructure, housing, and employment systems, as evidenced in various developing nations. Strategic planning and investment are therefore essential to ensure that population growth translates into sustainable development.
